GRC from zero — what it actually is, and what it is for them

1. The three letters, in your language

GRC = Governance, Risk, and Compliance. It’s not a technology — it’s the management system a security organization runs on, the same way a plant runs on its Quality Management System.

Leg What it means Your world
Governance The written rules: who’s allowed to do what, who approves what, what the standards are The plant’s SOPs and quality manual; the Army’s aviation regs — you revised one (USAACE Reg 350-3)
Risk Systematically finding what can go wrong, how bad, how likely, and deciding what you’ll accept vs. fix FMEA (Failure Modes and Effects Analysis) on a process; the pre-mission risk assessment worksheet where higher risk needs a higher-ranking signature
Compliance Proving, with evidence, that you actually follow the rules — to auditors who check OSHA audits, ISO certification audits, checkrides. Not “are you safe” but “can you PROVE it to the examiner”

Inside a federal CISO (Chief Information Security Officer) shop, a “GRC program” is the team and process that: writes the security policies (governance), runs every IT system through a formal risk-approval process before and while it operates (risk), and produces the evidence and scorecards that prove it to inspectors — DHS Office of Inspector General (OIG), GAO (Government Accountability Office), Congress (compliance).

SAY THIS (if asked “what does GRC mean to you?”): “Honestly? It’s a quality management system pointed at security instead of product. Governance is the quality manual, risk is the FMEA, compliance is the audit. I’ve spent my whole career inside that triangle — I revised our command’s aviation standards regulation, I ran hazard analysis and OSHA programs at a plant, and I sat through the audits. The subject matter here is new to me. The system is not.”

2. GRC specifically in the Coast Guard CISO shop — the machine Pete lives in

Here’s the actual machinery, one layer at a time:

The operating process: RMF. The Risk Management Framework, NIST Special Publication 800-37 Revision 2 (Rev 2 is current — published Dec 2018, still the standard in 2026). Seven steps: Prepare → Categorize → Select → Implement → Assess → Authorize → Monitor. Translation: figure out how important the system is, pick the safety rules that apply, install them, have an independent examiner check, get the boss’s signature to operate, then keep watching. Every one of the Coast Guard’s IT systems must go through this loop.

The rulebook: NIST SP 800-53. The master catalog of security controls — over a thousand controls and enhancements covering everything from password rules to visitor logs to backup power. Say “Rev 5” (that’s what a CISSP expects to hear); if precision ever comes up, the current patch is Release 5.2.0, August 2025 (it added software-update-integrity controls after a 2025 Executive Order — you don’t need more than that sentence). Systems don’t implement all of it — they get a baseline: Low = 149 controls, Moderate = 287, High = 370 (from companion doc SP 800-53B). Most systems are Moderate. Remember “287” — dropping that number naturally is instant credibility.

The deliverable: the ATO package. Authorization to Operate — the Authorizing Official’s (AO’s) formal signature that a system may run. To get it, someone assembles the System Security Plan (SSP — how each control is implemented), the assessment results, and the POA&M (Plan of Action and Milestones — the punch list of known gaps with owners and due dates). Your analogy: the AO is the commander signing the risk assessment worksheet before a marginal-weather launch — he’s personally accepting the residual risk, so the package has to make that risk honest and legible.

The system of record: eMASS (Enterprise Mission Assurance Support Service, “ee-mass”) — the Defense Information Systems Agency’s web application where DoD-side ATO packages live. Controls get checked off, artifacts uploaded, workflow routed AO-ward. Keep the prior brief’s hedge: “the system of record I understand you use” — don’t claim hands-on.

The hardening checklists: STIGs (Security Technical Implementation Guides, “stigs”) — DISA-published configuration checklists per product (Windows Server, Cisco routers, databases…), hundreds of individual settings each. Your analogy: the maintenance manual’s torque-spec tables — per-machine, exact, checkable. Much STIG checking is scanner-automatable (via SCAP — Security Content Automation Protocol); much of the rest is still done by hand.

The weird org-chart fact (know it, don’t lecture him on it): the Coast Guard is a DHS component, but its networks ride on the DoD Information Network (DODIN), so it follows DoD’s cyber rules — DoD RMF, eMASS, STIGs. Per a DHS OIG report, DHS formally accepted in May 2020 that the CG reports FISMA (Federal Information Security Modernization Act — the law requiring all of this) per DoD reporting requirements rather than DHS’s. Practical takeaway for you: this shop speaks DoD dialect while sitting inside DHS, and Pete’s team lives at that awkward seam. If it comes up, a good listening move: “You sit at a DHS–DoD seam — does that mean double reporting, or does the DoD side win?”

The AI program that makes your resume relevant: “RMF Automation.” Verified live in the DHS AI Use Case Inventory (USCG page, dhs.gov/ai/use-case-inventory/uscg): an AI tool that “will reduce processing times by more than half, rapidly generate core risk management documents, and shorten assessment activities to weeks,” with “near-real-time, role-specific risk insights,” explicitly “anchored in human judgment for key ATO decisions,” continuously monitoring controls and flagging issues. 3. The two manual tasks the JD names — what they look like done by hand

Pete’s JD names “evidence collection” and “control selection” as the manual grind to re-engineer. Automating these IS the job. Here’s the vivid picture, so you can describe his team’s Tuesday better than most candidates could.

Evidence collection — the screenshots-into-Word reality. Every control in the package needs an artifact — proof it’s actually implemented. Who does it: the ISSO (Information System Security Officer — the person who owns one system’s security paperwork), begging system admins for their time. What it physically looks like:

  • Screenshot the password-policy config screen — with hostname and timestamp visible, or the assessor rejects it. Paste into Word. Caption it. Save as “AC-2_evidence_v3_FINAL2.docx.” Upload to eMASS against the control.
  • Export the user-account list to prove no orphaned accounts. Pull vulnerability-scan results. Photocopy the visitor log. Chase down the training-completion roster. Screenshot the backup job. Hundreds of times.
  • Each control decomposes into multiple assessable line items (DoD tracks these as CCIs — Control Correlation Identifiers), so a 287-control Moderate system means thousands of individual proof-points.
  • The same evidence gets captured separately for every system’s package — ten systems on the same network means the same firewall proof harvested by hand ten times, because each package must stand alone.
  • Evidence expires: by the time the assessor reviews the package months later, half the screenshots are stale and get re-taken. Rework loop, forever.

Your play-back line: “So an ISSO’s job is partly taking timestamped screenshots of settings a script could read directly, pasting them into Word, and doing it again when they go stale — per system, per control. That’s not a judgment task, that’s a data-collection task done by hand.” That sentence tells Pete you get it.

Control selection — the giant-spreadsheet reality. Before evidence, someone decides WHICH controls apply. By hand it looks like: categorize the system (FIPS 199 — Federal Information Processing Standard 199 — rate the impact of losing confidentiality, integrity, availability); pick the baseline (149/287/370); then tailor — add overlays (extra control packs for privacy, classified data, etc.), mark which controls are inherited from the hosting environment (the data center’s fire suppression covers you — “common controls”), justify every not-applicable, and then write an implementation statement for each remaining control: a paragraph, in prose, in the SSP, saying how this system meets it. Person + Word/Excel + weeks. And most of those paragraphs are near-copies of paragraphs written for the last ten systems — which is precisely why a retrieval-augmented model with citations is so obviously the fix, and why Pete wrote “control selection” into the JD.

SAY THIS (the empathy + pattern-match move): “From what I’ve read, the grind is that evidence collection is a manual data-acquisition problem and control selection is a manual retrieval-and-drafting problem. I’ve automated both patterns before — I stopped humans hand-transcribing plant data and piped it straight from source systems, and my LLM pipeline did first-pass drafting against a knowledge base with the expert keeping the call. Cut review time 75%. Same two patterns, different domain.”

4. Why GRC is famously miserable — the pain inventory

Have these on tap; naming a pain accurately is how an outsider earns trust fast.

  • ATOs take 6–18 months and can cost six to seven figures per system; GSA’s 18F famously showed the process could drop from 6 months to 30 days with focused ATO sprints and pre-built platform inheritance — proving the delay is process, not physics.
  • A point-in-time snapshot, good for ~3 years — the day after signature, the paperwork starts rotting while the real system keeps changing. Compliant-on-paper ≠ secure-today. (This is exactly what continuous ATO — cATO, in the prior brief — exists to fix.)
  • Duplicated everything — same evidence, same prose, re-produced per package because packages can’t share data.
  • The people cost: talented security folks spending their week as clerks — screenshotting, formatting, chasing signatures — instead of analyzing risk. Morale and retention problem, not just a speed problem.
  • Backlog physics: modernization is throttled by authorization — new capability waits in the ATO queue, so the compliance process itself becomes the constraint on the mission.

1. The problem RAG solves — one breath

SAY THIS (memorize verbatim):

“A large language model knows what was on the public internet when it was trained. It doesn’t know your policies, your systems, or last month’s memo — and when it doesn’t know, it guesses confidently. And you can’t just paste your whole document library into every question, because the model can only read a limited amount at once. RAG fixes both: before the model answers, it looks up the most relevant passages from your own documents, answers only from those, and shows its receipts.”

One sentence version if he wants it shorter: “RAG is open-book test instead of closed-book — and the book is yours, and it has to cite the page.”

2. The pipeline — every step, and WHY each exists

The single most important structural idea — say this first, because it instantly sounds like someone who has built one:

“There are two halves: what you do once to the library (and keep fresh), and what happens every time someone asks a question.”

BUILD TIME (the library):

Step What happens WHY it exists
1. Ingest Collect the documents — PDFs, policies, logs, database exports — and convert them to plain text The model can’t read what you never collected. Garbage in, garbage out — same as any data pipeline
2. Chunk Split each document into passages, roughly a few paragraphs each You retrieve pieces, not whole 400-page documents. A whole document is too big to hand the model and too vague to match a specific question
3. Embed Run each chunk through an embedding model that turns its meaning into a long list of numbers (a vector) Computers can’t compare meaning directly, but they can compare numbers. This turns “find text about the same topic” into math
4. Store Save vector + original text + metadata (source doc, version, date, access tags) in a vector database So you can find the nearest-in-meaning chunks in milliseconds across millions of them — and the metadata is what makes citations and access filtering possible later

QUERY TIME (every question):

Step What happens WHY it exists
5. Embed the question Same embedding model turns the user’s question into a vector Must be the same model as step 3 — otherwise the question and the documents are on different maps and distances mean nothing
6. Similarity search The vector database finds the chunks whose vectors sit closest to the question’s vector “Closest in number-space” = “closest in meaning.” This is the retrieval in retrieval-augmented generation
7. Take top-k Keep the best 3–10 matches The model has limited reading space per question — you hand it the five most relevant pages, not the whole binder
8. Build the prompt Assemble: instructions + those retrieved chunks + the user’s question, with the rule “answer ONLY from these passages and cite which one” This is the grounding. The model is now doing reading comprehension on your documents, not recalling the internet
9. LLM answers, with citations Model writes the answer, naming its source chunks Receipts. If it can’t cite it, it doesn’t say it
10. Human validates Expert reviews before anything official happens The model does assembly; a person owns the judgment call. (Pete’s own “RMF Automation” tool works exactly this way)

The 60-second spoken narration (practice out loud with the drawing):

“Top row is the library, done once and kept fresh. I take the documents, split them into passages a few paragraphs long, run each passage through an embedding model — that converts its meaning into a list of numbers — and store the numbers, the original text, and the metadata like source and version in a vector database.

Bottom row is a question. I embed the question with the same model, so it lands on the same map. The database finds the handful of passages whose numbers sit closest — closest in numbers means closest in meaning. I take the top five, put them into a prompt that says ‘answer only from these passages and cite which one you used,’ and the model writes the answer with receipts. A human reviews it before it becomes anything official.

The model never answers from memory — it answers from what we retrieved. That’s what kills the making-things-up problem, and the citation trail is what makes it auditable.”

3. The vocabulary — dummy level, one analogy each

Term Plain English Your-world analogy
Embedding Meaning turned into a long list of numbers (a vector — think a few hundred to a few thousand numbers). Texts with similar meaning get similar numbers A grid coordinate for meaning. Two incident reports about the same failure mode land in nearby grid squares even if they use different words
Vector database A database organized by meaning instead of by keyword or part number. You ask “what’s near this?” and it answers fast, even across millions of entries A parts warehouse where similar parts are physically shelved together — you walk to one shelf and everything you need is within arm’s reach
Chunking Cutting documents into passages before embedding. Chunk too big → the vector is a mushy average of many topics and matches nothing well; too small → you retrieve a sentence with no context. Overlap means neighboring chunks share some text so a thought split across a boundary isn’t lost Overlap is a shift handoff: the off-going and on-coming shifts cover the same 15 minutes so nothing falls in the gap between them
Cosine similarity The math for “how close in meaning”: measures whether two vectors point the same direction. Score near 1 = same meaning, near 0 = unrelated Two aircraft headings. Same heading = same direction of travel; 90 degrees off = going different places. It compares direction, not distance flown
Top-k Keep only the k best matches (k = 3, 5, 10 — you tune it). Too few → you miss the answer; too many → you bury the model in noise Pulling the 5 relevant pages from the maintenance binder instead of handing the mechanic the whole binder
Context window The model’s total reading-plus-writing space for one exchange. Big nowadays — hundreds of pages — but never “your entire document library,” and models get worse at using what’s buried in the middle of a huge stack The kneeboard. Limited space; you put the approach plate you need on it, not the entire flight-information library
Hallucination The model states something false, fluently and confidently, because it’s completing plausible text — not looking anything up A pilot reporting position from memory instead of reading the instruments. Sounds confident; may be wrong; kills people
Grounding Forcing answers to come only from retrieved source text, with “say you don’t know” when retrieval comes back empty Instruments-only flying. You answer from what the gauges show, not from what you feel
Citations Every claim in the answer names the exact source chunk — document, section, version A maintenance log. No entry, no credit: if the action isn’t signed off against a source, it didn’t happen
Reranking A second, smarter, slower pass that re-scores the top ~50 fast matches and reorders them so the truly best 5 go to the model Sampling inspection finds the candidate lots fast; then you do 100% inspection on just the shortlist
Hybrid search Run keyword search AND vector search, merge results. Vector search is great at concepts but surprisingly bad at exact codes — control IDs like “AC-2(3)”, CVE numbers, part numbers. Keyword search nails those Vector = “find failures like this one.” Keyword = “find part number 70351-08102-043 exactly.” A real system needs both — and in their world, where everything is an exact control ID, hybrid isn’t optional
Evals A graded test set — questions with known-correct answers — run against the system before you trust it and re-run after every change to catch regressions The checkride. Nobody flies passengers because they “seem good”; they fly a graded standard course. Change the aircraft config, you re-evaluate

The hybrid-search row is your quiet ace: volunteering “pure vector search is weak on exact identifiers like control numbers, so for a control catalog I’d run hybrid — keyword plus semantic” is the single most builder-credible sentence in this whole section.

2. CI/CD — the robot and its checklist

CI — Continuous Integration: every time anyone proposes a change, a robot automatically runs a checklist of checks against it — within minutes, every time, no exceptions. Fail a check, the change is blocked from merging.

CD — Continuous Delivery/Deployment: once checks pass and the change merges, the robot automatically publishes or deploys the result. No human manually copying files to a server.

A “pipeline” is the robot’s checklist — the ordered list of steps (build it, test it, scan it, publish it). Here’s the elegant part: the pipeline is defined in a plain-text file that lives in the same repo, so the checklist itself is version-controlled and reviewed like everything else. The poka-yoke is itself a controlled document.

Names to recognize (don’t claim depth): GitHub Actions and GitLab CI/CD are the built-in pipeline robots of those two platforms; Jenkins is the old self-hosted workhorse you’ll still find in government.

Your analogy, use it: a pipeline is an in-line quality gate, not end-of-line inspection. Or in aviation terms: a pre-takeoff checklist that runs itself on every single flight and physically refuses to release brakes if an item fails. Nobody “forgets” the check, nobody pencil-whips it.

SAY THIS (CI/CD, ~20 seconds): > “CI/CD is just an automated quality gate. Every proposed change triggers a robot that runs the full checklist — tests, scans, formatting — and a failed check physically blocks the change from going in. Then it auto-publishes what passed. Changes go through branch → pull request → review → merge, exactly like code. 3. The pipeline auto-publishes on every merge — typically rebuilding an internal website (tools in this space: MkDocs, Hugo — recognition level only) so the published version is always the current approved revision. 4. Policy-as-Code (PaC) — policy a machine can enforce

A traditional policy is prose in a PDF: “encrypt data at rest; no publicly accessible storage.” It depends on humans reading, remembering, and periodically auditing it. Policy-as-code rewrites the rule in a form a machine evaluates automatically — usually as a gate in the pipeline.

The canonical example: “No public S3 buckets” (S3 = Amazon Simple Storage Service — cloud file storage; a “public bucket” is the classic accidental data leak). As a PDF, someone violates it in month three and you find out at audit time — end-of-line inspection, scrap already made. As code, the rule runs against every proposed infrastructure change before it happens, and a violating change is simply blocked. In-line poka-yoke; the defect never gets built.

This works because modern infrastructure is itself defined in text files — Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC), with Terraform as the mainstream tool. Servers, storage, and networks are described in code and stand up automatically, which means a policy engine can read the proposed change and pass/fail it before anything real changes.
