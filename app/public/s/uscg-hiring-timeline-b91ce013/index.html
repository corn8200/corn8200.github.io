01

Where it actually sits

Decoding Pete's two emails against how a federal hiring action really moves.

Pete wrote on 11 July: "I was unable to meet up with my boss (Chrishan)… I will be trying to figure out our hiring (offer) process on this. I will move as quickly as I can. Our HR and Gov in general can be slow."

Then on 20 July: "I have worked with a number of folks and we have the process going. You should hear from HR 'soon'."

Read together, those nine days were Pete getting Chrishan's concurrence, confirming the billet and its funding, and getting a Request for Personnel Action (SF-52) into the servicing HR office. "A number of folks" is the tell: this action needs more signatures than he expected, which is also why he called it "genuinely a different path for a hiring action for me."

He is almost certainly describing Executive Order 14356. The 2025 blanket hiring freeze lapsed on 15 October 2025 and was replaced the same day by a permanent regime: "No Federal civilian position that is vacant may be filled, and no new position may be created, except as provided for in this order or required by applicable law." Every agency stands up a Strategic Hiring Committee — deputy-secretary and chief-of-staff level, majority non-career — that must approve each vacancy and is explicitly told not to "ministerially ratify or routinely defer to the recommendations of others." Pete wrote a brand-new PD, which is the most committee-triggering flavor of action there is.

There are four ways out of that gate, not one — and the briefing most people give you names only the first. Sec. 3(a)(vi): the order does not apply to "positions related to immigration enforcement, national security, or public safety." Sec. 3(c): hiring specifically approved by the head of an executive department — DHS is one. Sec. 3(e): the OPM Director may grant exemptions, and exemptions previously granted under the 2025 freeze memoranda "shall remain in effect unless withdrawn by OPM" — DHS may already hold a grandfathered standing exemption, which is the path nobody talks about. Failing all three, committee approval. Note that nothing exempts a hire merely because the appointing authority is noncompetitive — 3112 gets no carve-out of its own; the order restricts filling the position.

A PD is not a billet. Pete writing a job description around your resume proves intent, not funding. If no funded billet exists behind it, this is a position creation under Sec. 2 — the most approval-triggering flavor of action there is — and the timeline is a quarter, not weeks.

One more thing worth naming plainly: Dale Perez is more than the door-opener. He signs as a Human Resources Specialist, and his directorate — CG-WMC, "Director Civilian Personnel" — develops Coast Guard civilian compensation policy and adjudicates compensation claims and waivers, classification appeals, and leave and benefits claims within delegated authority. That is precisely the list of things you need. Grade, series, ladder, and pay-setting questions belong with him, not with Pete, who told you honestly that his only lever is the PD.

Position classificationLiveSets everything
HR Specialist (classifier), PSC-CPM-1

What Pete wrote is a job description. A position description is a classified document. A classifier — not Pete, not you — evaluates the written duties against OPM standards and assigns pay plan, title, series, and grade. The Coast Guard's own guidance: a duty controls the series or grade only if it is regularly assigned for 25% or more of work time and requires a higher level of knowledge and skill. USCG PDs "normally include four to five major duty statements."

Duration
2–6 weeks typical for a new PD; longer if it bounces back for rewrite. Existing shelf PD: near zero.
Risk
The classifier can knock the grade down. If the written duties read GS-11, you get a GS-11 offer regardless of what Pete intended. There is also a live risk the classifier substitutes a generic 2210 PD from the automated classification module and your tailored language never reaches the record.
Fix the PD
Your seven-duty draft is a problem of form, not substance. Percentages need not be equal — 30/25/15/10/8/7/5 is fine — but each duty needs an explicit percentage, and the grade-controlling ones must individually carry 25% or more. The Coast Guard's rule is a two-prong test and the second prong is where the grade actually lives: the duty must also "require a higher level knowledge and skill in order to perform the work." A 30%-of-time duty written in routine-execution language is not grade-controlling. Write the AI-governance and policy-as-code duties in higher-level-knowledge language.
Careful
Per USCG, none of these affect the classified grade: your education, your certifications, difficulty recruiting, how well you'd perform, length of service, or clearance. Your GS-13 crosswalk and your Six Sigma belt have zero effect here. Management's intent does count — but for series, not grade, so reason-for-existence language helps on the 2210-vs-0343 question and does nothing on grade.
Lever
Ask whether this is a new PD or an existing classified one. And if it goes 2210: OPM issued a new Competency Based Position Classification Standard for the 2210 series on 13 April 2026 that replaced the old Job Family Standard and explicitly added "occupational distinctions between IT and non-IT work" — the exact boundary at issue for you. The legacy standard's URL now 404s. Anyone drafting factor language against the 2001 standard is drafting to a dead document.

What the numbers actually say

OPM's own 14-step model, with the announcement and certificate steps stripped out because they do not apply here, totals 32 calendar days. Government-wide time-to-hire for the 2210 family runs 94 days. DHS's own internal target is 150 days. And the widely quoted 80-day standard is measured to initial offer, not to your first day — anyone citing it as "80 days to start work" is wrong.

Why HR is slow right now

This is not Pete being pessimistic. DHS was shut down for 76 days this year — appropriations lapsed 14 February and were not restored until P.L. 119-86 was signed 30 April 2026. That followed a 43-day government-wide shutdown last autumn. The servicing HR office, the classifiers, and the security center are all still clearing that backlog.

The 30 September problem

Coast Guard salary money under P.L. 119-86 is a FY2026 annual appropriation that expires 30 September 2026. An action against money that already exists is far easier to defend than one that needs FY2027 money that does not yet exist. E.O. 14356 compliance is also measured by fiscal year — a billet inside the FY2026 staffing plan is cheap to execute now and gets re-litigated if it slips.

Meanwhile FY2027 is not settled. As of 21 July the Senate had passed zero of twelve appropriations bills. A clean continuing resolution through 4 December 2026 (H.R. 9770) was on the House floor schedule for the week of 20–23 July. A CR is survivable for an in-flight hire; a lapse is not — and this department has already produced one lapse this fiscal year.

This gives you the single best question you can ask, and it reads as helpful rather than pushy: "Is this billet funded on FY26 money, and does an EOD before 30 September protect it?" That reframes your follow-up from checking in to helping Pete beat his own deadline.

04

What becomes unfixable at entrance on duty

Five determinations. All discretionary, none appealable, every one of them dead the morning you report. Ranked by cost of missing it.

The step5 CFR 531.212(e)(1)

"An agency must approve each determination to use the superior qualifications and special needs pay-setting authority prior to the candidate entering on duty." OPM's own fact sheet is blunter: "The determination to set pay above step 1 cannot be made retroactively." There is no cure, no back pay, no reconsideration.

Approval must be in writing by an official at least one level higher than your supervisor. Pete cannot approve it — he has to carry it upward. He was half right when he said pay is "HR's call": HR documents it, but line management decides it.

Worth $17,070 in year one · ~$109,000 over ten years
The appointment type — and whether you get FERS at all5 CFR 316.403 · 842.105

3112 cannot give you a permanent appointment on day one. The statute authorizes "a noncompetitive appointment leading to conversion." You will start on either a term appointment (over 1 year, up to 4 — up to 10 for the 2200 series group) or a temporary one (1 year or less).

That distinction is not cosmetic. A temporary appointment limited to a year or less is excluded from FERS — no retirement credit, no agency automatic 1%, no TSP match, no FEGLI. A term appointment carries all of it. It also decides whether you accrue MSPB adverse-action rights at the one-year mark.

The fix is a single sentence of regulation. 5 CFR 316.403(b)(1) names disabled-veteran 3112 appointments by name as eligible for provisional designation, which restores FERS, FEHB with the government contribution, and TSP. But 316.403(a)(3) requires the agency's intent to convert be stated in the written offer of employment. If that sentence is not in your offer letter, ask for it before you sign — retrofitting it afterward is far harder.

One trap to be ready for: 316.403(b)(1) cross-references "§ 316.402(b)(5)," but the disabled-veteran provision was renumbered and now sits at 316.402(b)(4). A careful HR specialist following the citation literally lands on the wrong paragraph and may tell you provisional does not apply. Its own text says otherwise.

Leave service credit5 CFR 630.205 · 5 U.S.C. 6303(a)

Two separate grants, both requiring written approval before you enter on duty, and both routinely under-asked.

Campaign service. As a retiree your twenty years generally do not count toward leave accrual — except service in a war or in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge was authorized. Your roughly 3 years 8 months should move you from 4 hours per pay period to 6, on day one. Bring a one-page schedule of qualifying deployments with inclusive dates cross-referenced to award citations; if your DD-214 shows only ribbons without date ranges, the specialist will default to zero.

The bigger one. 5 CFR 630.205(b) lets an agency credit active duty that is otherwise non-creditable, for a military retiree, where the experience directly relates to the position. Paragraph (a) does the same for private-sector work. Reaching 15 years of credited service puts you at 8 hours per pay period — 208 hours a year instead of 104.

Also check whether your Army retirement was in any part a disability retirement traceable to armed conflict or an instrumentality of war. If so, all twenty years count automatically. A 100% VA rating does not establish this — your retirement orders do.

Worth $3,206–$5,954 per year, permanently
The Bronze Belt correction5 CFR 731.202(b)(3) · 731.103(f)

The submitted resume says Six Sigma Green Belt. The truth is Bronze. This is the only self-inflicted risk in the entire file, and it sits in a legally distinct category from everything else.

A material, intentional false statement in examination or appointment is a suitability factor — and 5 CFR 731.103(f) reserves to OPM, not the agency, sole jurisdiction over such a case, with mandatory referral and debarment exposure. OPM may act "at any point when information giving rise to such a charge is discovered." A new suitability rule effective 30 July 2026 extends these actions to conduct discovered after appointment, so the exposure does not end when you report.

Separately: if you accept a recruitment incentive and this later surfaces, 5 CFR 575.111(j) requires repayment of the entire incentive — no proration, no waiver.

The certification is not a qualification requirement for a 3112 appointment. Correcting it costs you nothing on quals and converts a potential integrity finding into a clerical footnote. Do it in writing, to Perez and to HR, before the OF-306 and before your packet reaches leadership review. Self-reported today it is a non-event; discovered later it is not.

Clearance reciprocity — stop assuming it lapsedSEAD 7

The "24-month rule" everyone repeats is a DoD implementing rule. The Coast Guard is DHS. SEAD 7, which governs, contains no such rule — and its footnote 3 states expressly that an administrative termination because someone "is no longer affiliated with the U.S. Government" is not a denial, revocation, or suspension.

What actually controls is the age of your last Army investigation. If it closed in 2019 or later, it is inside SEAD 7's seven-year window and the Coast Guard is required to accept it absent new derogatory information. And USCG is genuinely good at this: in FY2022 it processed 380 reciprocity approvals at a median of five to six days — second fastest in DHS.

Have ready: full legal name and any former names, SSN, date and place of birth, dates of active service, the date and type of your last background investigation, the date and level of your last eligibility determination and the adjudicating facility, your last unit and its security manager, and copies of the DD-214 Member-4 and any SF-312 you signed. Handing the security office those identifiers is the difference between an autumn start and a 2027 start.

The argument you cannot make

Since the 2024 pay-equity amendment, 5 CFR 531.212(c)(2)(ix) bars the agency from considering "the candidate's salary history (i.e., existing salary or prior salary) or a salary from a competing job offer." OPM adds that agencies should not even request non-federal salary information.

Your $169,200 is legally inert. "I'd be taking a $50k pay cut" cannot lawfully move the step and can make the file look defective. Decline to provide salary history if asked.

What is admissible — 5 CFR 531.212(c). One factor is mandatory: the step at which similarly qualified people were recently appointed to similar positions. Then one or more of: the level and quality of your skills; documented disparities between federal and non-federal salaries for those skills (aggregate market data — BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics for the Washington MSA is expressly the right kind of evidence, and OPM advises using more than one source); labor-market conditions; recent recruiting failures; turnover; and the criticality of the position and the cost of not filling it. OPM: "Utilizing as many factors … as are applicable to the situation will strengthen the justification."

Your criticality argument writes itself, and it is in the budget. The FY2027 President's Budget carries a dedicated Zero Trust program change — +$31.0M and 10 positions — justified by the need to "ensure the Service's systems maintain their Authorizations to Operate." Total Coast Guard cyber funding rises from $291.57M to $357.61M, a 22.6% increase, with the NIST Govern function specifically going from $20.67M to $25.05M. Reporting on the Force Design 2028 Technology Campaign describes a hard September 2027 zero-trust deadline across more than 100 applications, with roughly 50 more coming. And the Coast Guard's own Digital Transformation Strategy of 12 May 2026 describes the service as operating at a "low digital maturity level" with heavy reliance on "paper-based workflows and static PDFs."

That is the entire case: a fixed 2027 compliance cliff, across 100+ applications, in an organization that has publicly described itself as running on PDFs, with no way to get there manually. Nothing in that paragraph mentions what you currently earn.

And here is the timing correction that matters most. Do not wait for the tentative offer and then react to a step-1 number. By then the offer package is built, and you are asking a completed file to run backwards through an approving official who has never met you. Write the justification yourself, now, and hand Pete finished work. He cannot approve it — but he is the one who has to initiate it and route it upward, and handing him a completed 1–2 page package structured to the 531.212(c) factors removes the single most common reason these never happen.

Honest probability, and this is judgment rather than data — there is no published USCG approval rate. Roughly 35–50% if you hand Pete a finished package in the next two weeks. Materially lower if you wait and react to a step-1 offer. In favor: 2210 cyber is a recognized shortage category with real precedent; C5ISC already pays incentives at this duty station; waiver authority moved in-house in February; the criticality case is in the President's Budget; and Pete wrote the PD around you, so he wants this to close. Against: it is wholly discretionary with no appeal right, it needs a signature from someone who has never met you, your most intuitive argument is inadmissible, and a manager already stretched by an unfamiliar 3112 mechanic may not want to bolt a pay package onto it.

Say the floor out loud once, early, framed as planning rather than demand — something like: "So nobody's time gets wasted, the number I can make work is GS-12 step 6, or the GS-13 grade. I've put together the market justification your HR shop would need for the superior-qualifications file if that's useful." If step 6 is genuinely a walk-away, telling Pete in August is a kindness. Telling him in November, after three months of his work, damages a relationship you will need on day one.

Two levers that are dead — do not raise them. There is no GS-2210 special salary rate at GS-12 in the DC locality in 2026 (the government-wide IT table tops out at GS-11 in CONUS). And the Maximum Payable Rate rule cannot help you: "highest previous rate" means pay from a civilian federal position, so CW4 pay does not qualify. Raising either one costs you credibility with HR.

The recruitment incentive is a legitimate parallel ask, not a substitute. The regulation itself forces the conversation — 531.212(e)(2)(iii) requires the file to document why a higher step was chosen "instead of or in addition to" an incentive. The comparator posting at this exact duty station authorized 10%, so C5ISC is already comfortable with the mechanism. The cap is 25% of annual basic pay per year of service agreement, and as of 13 February 2026 the authority to waive that cap moved from OPM to the agency itself — which removes the standard "we'd have to ask OPM" excuse. But the incentive is one-time cash: it does not raise your base, your high-3, or your TSP match. Take both; never trade the step for the bonus.

The ask that beats the step fight

The cheapest route to your floor may not be a contested pay memo at all.

Because this is a noncompetitive appointment with no announcement, no certificate, and no rating panel, there is no cert-limited grade ceiling. The agency may appoint at any grade you qualify for, and the classified grade of the PD sets the range. OPM's own guidance for this authority states there is no grade level limitation.

Look at what that means against your floor. GS-13 step 1 is $121,785 — more than GS-12 step 6 at $119,485. A GS-13 classification clears your stated floor at step 1, with no superior-qualifications memo, no approval one level above Pete, and no pay-equity argument to construct. The GS-13 ceiling is $158,322 against $133,142 at GS-12.

And a career ladder is a real, precedented ask at this exact duty station. It is true that most Coast Guard 2210 postings advertise "Promotion potential: None" — but not all of them. Announcement 20240805-88003-048437VP-DHA, IT Cyber Spec (PLCYPLN-INFOSEC) in the C5ISC Engineering Services Division, listed duty locations of Portsmouth VA or Kearneysville WV, at GS 12–13 with promotion potential to 13: "If this position is filled at a level below the full performance level, the selectee may be promoted without further competition."

That single announcement does three things at once: it refutes "USCG doesn't ladder 2210s," it refutes "Direct Hire Authority means terminal grade" (it was a DHA announcement with a ladder — DHA governs how you compete, not the position's full performance level), and it covers Kearneysville. Worth knowing too: "Promotion potential: None" is the full-performance-level field, which USCG HR fills inconsistently — some single-grade GS-12 postings say "None," others say "12," meaning the identical thing.

Ask Pete sequentially, not simultaneously:

1. "Can the PD be established at GS-12/13 with promotion potential to 13?" — and hand him announcement 20240805-88003-048437VP-DHA.  2. If no: "Will you route a superior-qualifications package to the approving official for step 6? I've drafted the justification to the 531.212(c) factors — here it is."

Best combined outcome: a GS-12/13 ladder PD, entered at GS-12 step 6, promoting to GS-13 step 3. The honest counterweight is calendar — pushing for GS-13 reopens classification and adds time.

Note the tension worth understanding before you push on series: 0343 is the easier qualification path, but 2210 is the recognized shortage category with far better precedent for special pay-setting, and it is how C5ISC classifies everything in this space. The series has zero pay consequence at GS-12 in this locality in 2026 — both pay the identical DCB rate. It matters for how hard you are to qualify, for the certifications the PD may impose, and for how long a term appointment can run before conversion. Don't spend much capital fighting for one.

The comparison you are actually making

Not step 6 versus step 1. $169,200 versus $119,485 — a gross delta of $49,715 a year.

Your Army retired pay and your VA compensation are constant on both sides of that ledger. Dual-compensation restrictions were repealed in 1999, so you draw full retired pay, full federal salary, and full tax-free VA either way. That kills the usual bad folklore given to retiring servicemembers — but it cuts the other direction too: because you have them regardless, they do not offset the pay cut. Don't let "total comp is fine" do work it can't do.

The genuine offsets to $49,715: roughly $5,974/yr in TSP employer contributions at step 6, FERS annuity accrual, the FEHB employer share, up to 208 hours a year of leave if the service credit lands, a twenty-minute commute, and stability. That is a defensible trade — and it is roughly a $40k/yr net one. Make it explicitly rather than drifting into it.

08

What could still kill it

Ranked by probability times impact. None of these is a reason to stop — all of them are reasons not to resign early.

High
The classifier lands it at GS-11, or in a series you struggle to qualify into
Grade and series are decided by a PSC-CPM-1 classifier reading the written duties — not by Pete's intent and not by how the interview went. A 2210 determination invites the question of whether your manufacturing and Six Sigma work was "primarily IT."
Mitigation Get the series confirmed through Perez now; offer the consolidated PD; your GS-12-equivalence crosswalk is the ammunition.
High
You EOD at step 1 because the offer moved faster than the pay memo
This is the most common way people lose this money. The TJO arrives with a step already computed, there is pressure to accept, the start date gets set, and the authority expires the morning you report.
Mitigation Have the memo drafted before the TJO exists. Tell HR plainly that you will accept once the step is on the offer. There is a thin fallback at conversion, but treat it as a fallback, not a plan.
Med
The vacancy sits in a Strategic Hiring Committee queue
A DHS board at deputy-secretary level, told explicitly not to rubber-stamp, has to approve creating and filling a brand-new position. Agencies may batch vacancies, so this can be pure queue time with no signal.
Mitigation Ask whether the national-security exception applies. If it does, this gate vanishes entirely.
Med
A full investigation instead of reciprocity
If the security office opens initial vetting rather than re-establishment of trust, you eat 109 days at moderate tier or 220 at high — and your own draft PD, describing privileged access to security controls in a CI/CD pipeline, is exactly the fact pattern that can push a position from noncritical- to critical-sensitive.
Mitigation Hand over your prior-investigation identifiers unprompted; ask for a preliminary determination and interim Secret; ask Pete what sensitivity the PD carries.
Med
Appropriations lapse on 1 October
The Senate has passed none of twelve FY2027 bills. A CR is the base case, but DHS already produced a 76-day lapse this fiscal year. During a lapse, security offices stop adjudicating and start dates slip by however long it runs.
Mitigation Push for a September EOD. Do not resign against a start date scheduled for early October.
Med
You start on a temporary appointment with no FERS and no conversion commitment
Conversion under 5 CFR 315.707 is permissive — no trigger, no deadline, no entitlement — and a term appointment "ends automatically on the expiration of his term appointment." Until conversion you hold no competitive status and sit in the lowest RIF retention group.
Mitigation Get provisional designation and the intent-to-convert sentence in the written offer. Ask for a stated conversion timeline — conversion can legally happen immediately, so a date is a negotiable term, not a legal constraint.
Low
Leadership transition re-baselines the priorities
The Assistant Commandant / PEO-C5I / CIO seat was listed as "SES/TBD" as of March 2026 and is being converted from a flag billet to a Senior Executive Service position. An incoming SES can revisit PDs and hiring plans.
Mitigation Nothing to do but move fast. It cuts both ways — an unsettled chain also gives Pete more latitude on the PD.
Low
CTAP documentation nobody has completed
ICTAP and the reemployment priority list both except 10-point preference eligibles. CTAP does not — and any appointment over 121 days is covered even with no announcement. It is a documentation step, not a competition, but the file cannot move until someone writes the negative finding.
Mitigation If things go quiet: "has CTAP clearance been documented?" is a precise question that gives someone something to unstick.

Silence protocol

Three weeks with no HR contact — go to Perez, not Pete, and ask him to check the action's status. He is HR and he owns this authority service-wide; he can see things Pete cannot.

Pete gets a light touch every two weeks at most, and the touch should carry information or a question that helps him, never "just checking in."

After the TJO, silence usually means the security file. The right question is where it sits at SECCEN, and PSC-CPM-1 is the office that can ask.

Read latency as system state, not as a verdict. DHS lost 76 working days this spring, the Coast Guard has publicly named civilian onboarding as a bottleneck it is trying to fix, and Pete has told you twice, in his own words, that this is unfamiliar ground for him. None of that is about you.
