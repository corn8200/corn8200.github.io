02

The vocabulary — decoded

Plain English + your hook into each. The modernization-edge terms at the bottom are the ones to volunteer — they make a non-cyber candidate sound fluent.

Core — know these so you don't blink
RMF
NIST SP 800-37 Rev 2
The government's 7-step process to make a system secure enough to run and keep it that way: Prepare → Categorize → Select → Implement → Assess → Authorize → Monitor. (Rev 2 is current; there's no Rev 3.) Your hook: a quality-management process with steps, controls, and continuous monitoring — Six Sigma DMAIC in a security costume.
ATO
Authorization to Operate · "AY-toe"
The senior official's formal sign-off that a system may run — traditionally a point-in-time approval good ~3 years. Getting one = assembling months of evidence. The bottleneck everyone wants automated. Your hook: the months-of-paperwork problem your 75% review-time pipeline attacks. Strongest bridge — §4.
ConMon
Continuous Monitoring · RMF step 7
After the ATO, keep watching that controls still work as configs change and new vulnerabilities appear — ideally automated telemetry, not an annual manual review. Your hook: a digital twin watching a live process for drift is the same mental model.
Controls
NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 (5.1.1)
The master catalog of security requirements (hundreds) a system must meet. RMF is mostly selecting, implementing, and proving controls. (Say "Rev 5" — precision matters to a CISSP.) Your hook: a spec with hundreds of line-items you audit against — like a quality standard or a flight-standards reg.
SSP / SAR / POA&M
the "authorization package"
SSP = System Security Plan (boundary, categorization, how each control is implemented). SAR = assessment results. POA&M = the tracked list of known gaps + fix dates. Together they're the package the AO reviews to grant an ATO. Your hook: SSP = the spec sheet; POA&M = a corrective-action log / punch list with owners and due dates.
eMASS
"ee-mass"
DISA's web system of record where DoD (and USCG, via DoD RMF) builds and holds ATO packages. The database everyone complains about doing by hand. Your hook: recognize it; say "the RMF system of record I understand you use" — don't claim hands-on depth a CISSP can probe.
Zero Trust
ZTA · two models exist
Stop trusting anything just because it's "inside" — verify every user and access, every time. Know both models: CISA (5 pillars: Identity, Devices, Networks, Apps & Workloads, Data) and DoD (7 pillars, FY2027 target). USCG follows the DoD track. Your hook: old = guarded fence, free movement inside; ZT = every door checks your badge. You set access-control standards — this is that at scale.
ISSO / ISSM
the traditional cyber roles
ISSO = owns one system's security posture + paperwork; ISSM = program-level policy/oversight. Your hook: Plezia said this won't be a standard ISSO interview — he's not hiring a paperwork-runner, he wants the person who automates the ISSO's grind. Don't try to out-ISSO an ISSO.
FISMA / AO / SCA
the law + the people
FISMA = the law requiring all this. AO (Authorizing Official) = the exec who accepts the risk and signs the ATO (older folks may say "DAA"). SCA = Security Control Assessor, the independent tester. Your hook: AO = the plant manager who signs off before the line runs.
Modernization edge — volunteer these to stand out
cATO
Continuous ATO
DoD's Feb-2022 construct: instead of a 3-year point-in-time ATO, keep a system continuously authorized by proving three things — continuous control monitoring, active real-time cyber defense, and an approved DevSecOps pipeline. No expiration, but revocable. Your hook: this is "automate the ATO + monitor forever" — exactly what the CG's RMF Automation tool moves toward. Volunteering "cATO" signals you read past the compliance surface.
CSRMC
DoD, launched Sep 2025
DoD's new Cybersecurity Risk Management Construct — modernizes/replaces traditional RMF and pushes toward continuous authorization (rolling evaluation, not a check every 3 years). Since USCG follows DoD RMF, this shapes their direction. Your hook: lets you ask whether their RMF-automation work is the on-ramp to cATO — reads the policy tea leaves.
OSCAL
NIST machine-readable standard
A NIST format (JSON/XML) that turns control catalogs, SSPs, and POA&Ms into machine-readable data so tools can automate compliance. NIST's stated goal: "audit durations from months to minutes" (real-world: weeks-to-days). Mature in the FedRAMP cloud world, emerging in DoD/eMASS. Your hook: it's a data format, not an engine — the plumbing that makes ATO automation possible. Frame as "where DoD/CG RMF is heading," not "what USCG runs today."
CDM
DHS/CISA program
DHS's Continuous Diagnostics & Mitigation — government-wide dashboards of cyber posture. GAO (Jun 2025) found data-quality gaps forcing manual dashboard updates at most agencies. USCG feeds these dashboards. Your hook: a real, current, public pain point that your data-pipeline strength speaks to directly.

The whiteboard case — a repeatable method, out loud

When he says "walk me through how you'd approach X," don't jump to an answer. Narrate this framework — it's a system-design rubric and Lean Six Sigma DMAIC. Name it "DMAIC" to signal method, not instinct.

Your repeatable approach — say each step aloud
clarify goals + constraints→ inventory current state & data→ isolate THE bottleneck→ low-risk pilot→ metrics + guardrails→ scale
  1. Ask before you answer. "What's the goal and the constraints?" State your assumptions aloud. Never present a finished design — federal interviewers score your reasoning, not a polished guess.
  2. Anchor in what you shipped. Your real example: siloed data → unified pipeline → knowledge base → LLM first-pass → expert validates → −75% review time. Then reframe it live: swap "job-hazard-analysis review" for "ATO package / ConMon evidence review" — same pattern.
  3. Draw the value stream (your ace). Current state — manual, serial, months — to future state — assisted, parallel, days. Mark exactly where time leaks. Most cyber folks have only followed the process; you can map and re-engineer it.
  4. Close on guardrails, not just speed. "And because I'd own the data-access and residency standards, it ships compliant by design." Then hand him the mic: "Is that the bottleneck you're trying to automate, or is the first fire somewhere else?"

Hard questions — and how to answer

Rehearse the first one out loud until it's ~45 seconds and automatic. It's the whole ballgame.

"You don't have a cyber background. Why should we hire you?"
Don't apologize, don't bluff. Reframe the gap as the reason: "You told me this isn't an ISSO/RMF-paperwork role — and that's exactly why I fit. Your mission is a data-integration, automation, and change-management problem: kill the siloed databases, stand up one integrated data environment across 350-plus systems, add AI, automate the ATO. That's what I just did at a nine-plant manufacturer — unified siloed SCADA/historian/quality data into one pipeline plus a knowledge base, built an LLM pipeline that cut review time 75%, and set the data-residency and access-control standards, for $240K in finance-verified savings. The RMF control depth is learnable, and you — a CISSP — already own it. What's hard to hire is someone who's actually shipped production AI and data integration in a regulated, safety-critical, multi-site org and driven adoption. And I'm not compliance-naive: as a standardization/instructor pilot I rewrote the command standards regulation and evaluated every crew to published standards — RMF is standards-to-practice, which is literally the job I did in the Army." The standards-officer bridge is the part no other candidate can offer — deliver it as the closer.
"Isn't AI risky / inaccurate / insecure for this?" — preempt this, in this order
A skeptic trusts the person who names the failure modes first. Volunteer, unprompted:
  1. Human-in-the-loop first — mirror their own tool's language: "the AI drafts and flags; the AO decides. Never autonomous authority over an ATO."
  2. Hallucination → grounding + citations: "it retrieves against authoritative artifacts and cites the source — if it can't cite the control, it doesn't assert it. Every output is auditable."
  3. Data security: "data boundary / residency, gov-cloud, least-privilege access to control data, models that don't train on it — I set exactly these standards before."
  4. The AI system is itself an asset under zero trust — put it under continuous monitoring like anything else.
Avoid hype words ("revolutionary," "fully automated"). Say "decision-support that compresses analyst time."
"How would you get up to speed on RMF / our environment?"
"The same way I learned nine plants cold — shadow the people doing the ATO grind for two weeks, map the current-state value stream myself, and find the two steps eating the most time. I learn a system by mapping it, under a lot more pressure than this." Humility + a concrete method beats hand-waving.
"Where do you see yourself / are you okay at GS-12?"
Signal you want to build, not title-climb — and keep grade talk away from Plezia: "I want to be the person shipping the modernization wins. If the work's real and the impact's measurable, I'm in." Series/grade/ladder/step → Perez & HR (§7, §9), never negotiated in the room.
Federal interview rules private-sector folks break
  • Say "I," not "we." A scoring rubric can't credit the team — only your specific action.
  • Quantify everything — $240K, 75%, 70.8%, $189K, zero recordables, 350 systems, 9 plants. Over-explain your action even when it feels excessive; panels score only what you explicitly say.
  • Different story per question. Recycling the AI story four times drops your score.
  • Anchor "how would you" answers in past tense — "here's how I actually did this," not aspiration.
  • Clearance precision: say "TS/SCI adjudicated 2020, inactive since my 2022 retirement, reinstatable on sponsorship." Never "I have an active TS/SCI" — overstating is a red flag to a CISSP.
  • Never badmouth an employer.
