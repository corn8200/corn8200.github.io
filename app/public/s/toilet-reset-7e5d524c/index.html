Setting the toilet back down

The bowl is already off the floor. This picks up from there and takes you to a sealed, non-rocking, leak-tested fixture. The whole job hinges on one rule: dry-fit before the wax touches anything.

Four questions before you buy anything

ten minutes here saves a second trip and a re-do

  1. Why did it come off in the first place?

    If it was leaking, rocking, or smelling before you pulled it, re-setting it on the same substrate reproduces the failure. That history tells you what to look for when the flange is bare.

  2. Has anyone added flooring since it was last set?

    Tile, LVP, underlayment — this is the #1 cause of a recessed flange, and it changes what you buy. Answer this before you read the flange table below, because it usually predicts the answer.

  3. Is there living space below this bathroom?

    If yes, you get a free leak detector: check that ceiling at 24 hours and again at one week. If it's a slab, you don't — lean harder on the paper-towel test.

  4. Test the shutoff valve — NOW, while the floor is clear

    Find and test your main house shutoff first. Then open the toilet's angle stop briefly into a bucket and confirm it fully closes and holds. Valves that haven't moved in years often weep or seize once disturbed.

    Why nowIf that stop needs replacing, doing it with a freshly-set toilet in the way is dramatically worse — and if it's a sweat-on valve you'd be running a torch six inches from porcelain.

Measure the flange height — this decides what you buy

Scrape enough old wax off to see bare metal or plastic, then lay a straightedge or torpedo level across the flange with both ends resting on the finished floor. Measure the gap at 3–4 points around it.

What you measureWhat you buy
Flush to 1/4" proudStandard wax ring. This is correct height — you're fine.
Flush or slightly belowExtra-thick wax ring — Oatey MaxWax 31188 or Fluidmaster 7513
More than 1/2" belowFlange extender kit — Oatey Set-Rite 43400 (corrects 1/8"–2-1/4") + a standard ring on top. Needs longer bolts.
1/4" to 1/2" proudWorkable. Use a thin / no-horn ring and expect to shim more than usual — the base sits higher relative to the floor.
More than 1/2" proudThe bowl can't seat on the floor — it bears on the flange rim. Real fixes are cutting the flange down or replacing it. This is a call-a-plumber line.
Readings differ by >1/8"The flange is tilted, not just high or low. Wax handles this better than any waxless seal. Plan on more shimming, and re-check the flange fasteners.
Do not stack two wax rings to make up height Oatey — who makes the rings — is flat about it: stacked rings compress unevenly, don't adhere to each other, and shear apart when the bowl moves. Use one correctly-sized ring or an extender. Honest note: working master plumbers do stack routinely and get away with it. It's a pro's daily judgment call, not a homeowner's one-shot default.

Two more things before the store

  1. Bring the wax ring inside — and warm the room

    Wax wants to be around 70°F (under 80°F) so it compresses instead of crumbles. An hour in the warm bathroom does it. A 70° ring pressed onto a 45° flange still seats badly, so warm the room, not just the ring.

    NeverNo hair dryer, heat gun, or microwave. A slumped ring looks fine and fails into your subfloor.
  2. Inspect the toilet itself

    A pulled toilet has usually been set down hard at least once. Check the bowl and horn for chips and hairline cracks, and the tank interior for cracks radiating from the bolt holes. Two minutes now beats finding it after the wax is committed.

The rule that governs the entire job

Dry-fit the bare bowl first. No wax.

Find every rock point. Mark the position on the floor. Mark where the shims go. Then lift it off and place the ring.

Wax has no memory. It seals by cold-flow — it squashes and stays squashed. It does not spring back. The moment the bowl touches the ring and you lift it, that ring is scrap.

So: buy two rings. They're $4. That's the difference between finishing today and a store run with an open sewer pipe in your bathroom.

02

Prep the opening

phase 1 · ~20 min · fully reversible

  1. Drain the toilet's trapway

    A pulled toilet still holds a quart or more of standing water in the trap. Tip it and lower it and that water runs straight out the horn onto your fresh wax — which is exactly what "contaminated seal" means. Sponge or shop-vac it out, tip the bowl into a bucket, wipe the horn dry.

    BonusThat's 8–10 lbs off the lift, which matters a lot when you're aiming it solo.
  2. Plug the drain

    The open drain is untrapped. Sewer gas comes up it, and it will swallow any nut, screw, or scraper you drop.

    • Best: mechanical test plug, wing nut snugged
    • Fallback: a rag too big to pass the pipe, with a long tail hanging visibly over the flange rim
    Write this on your handPull the plug before the ring goes down. A rag flushed into the closet bend goes where a plunger can't reach and an auger only drives deeper — that's a full re-pull of the toilet you just set.
  3. Scrape both surfaces

    Old wax comes off the flange AND the toilet's horn (the outlet on the underside of the bowl). Leftover nubs act as spacers that hold the bowl up off the new ring.

    • Pry the bulk off with the plastic putty knife, bag it immediately
    • Scrape to bare and dry — wax won't seat against a greasy film
    • Don't obsess. Get it bare enough to inspect — that's the real reason it matters. Wax hides cracks and broken bolt ears.
  4. Inspect the flange — your stop-work checkpoint
    What you findWhat to do
    Sound, intact bolt slotsProceed
    Missing or backed-out screwsThe common middle case. Drive stainless #10 × 1-1/2" into every open hole, into solid subfloor. On tile, pilot-drill first or you'll crack it.
    Cracked, or a broken bolt earFlange repair ring over the top — see the drop-down below
    Screws spin or pull out by handThe wood is gone. Longer screws don't fix it. Stop and fix the subfloor.
    Spongy floor, dark stains, musty smellSubfloor rot. Stop.
    Damaged drain section · cast iron · lead · slab🛑 Call a plumber. This is the line.
  5. Set the closet bolts and lock them upright

    Head into the flange slot, threads pointing up. Slide both to position — equidistant from the wall, on a line parallel to it. Measure it; flange slots are arcs and it's easy to set them out of square.

    You cannot align 55 lbs of porcelain onto bolts that flop over. Lock them:

    • Double-nut — washer + nut run down onto the flange and tightened. Permanent, ~$2, and the standard fix for a bolt that spins. Remember the length warning.
    • Or bolts with plastic retainer washers (Hercules Johni Quick Bolt, Sioux Chief PlumbPerfect)
    • Or pack scrap wax around the bolt bases — a real trade trick, easier to undo later than caulk
If the flange needs a repair ring or an extender

Repair ring (cracked flange / broken bolt ear) — it goes over the existing flange. Clear the broken ear first. Liberal bead of 100% silicone between the old flange and the new ring. Screws must reach solid subfloor, not just the plywood face. Re-square the new bolt slots to the wall before you commit.

Extender kit (recessed flange) — dry-stack the rings to height first and check. Then a continuous 100% silicone bead between every layer and between the bottom layer and the original flange. Fasten down through into subfloor. Longer bolts are mandatory. Let the silicone skin over before you set the bowl.

"Stop and fix the subfloor" — that job is: cut back to sound material, sister or replace the framing, re-support the flange. It's a legitimate call-a-pro moment and it is a different day's work. Don't set a toilet on rotten wood because you already bought the ring.

If the job stalls here Store run, missing part, whatever: plug the drain, ventilate, tell the household the water is off and the bathroom is out of service. Never leave an untrapped drain open overnight.
03

The dry fit

phase 2 · ~15 min · this is why the job works

  1. Lower the bare bowl onto the bare flange

    Over the standing bolts. No wax. This is your free rehearsal — everything you learn here you get to use once, for real, later.

    Lift correctlyLift the bowl only — never by the tank, the seat, or the flush lever. Take the seat and lid off first, and if the tank is still attached, seriously consider pulling it: that's ~20 lbs off and it restores your sightlines to the bolts. Set the tank lid flat on a towel — it's the most-broken part in this whole job.
  2. Find every rock point

    Push the base firmly front, back, left, and right. Then sit on it. You're looking for any lift, anywhere.

  3. Mark the position on the floor

    Pencil the base outline at 4–6 points, or run masking tape right up to the outer edge on both sides. This is your aiming target later, when you're lowering it blind. American Standard prints this exact tip on their install sheet.

  4. Shim until the rock is gone
    • Thin end first, into the widest part of each gap, until it's in firm contact with both floor and porcelain
    • 2–4 shims is normal. One per side is the usual fix.
    • Aim to keep the front of the bowl touching the floor — shims typically end up at the back, out of the splash zone
    • Push by hand only. Never tap them with a hammer.
    • Danco shims are ribbed to interlock, so two can legitimately stack for a deeper gap
    • Needing more than ~1/4"? Stop and re-check the flange. Something's wrong upstream.
  5. Mark the shim positions and test-thread a nut

    Pencil each shim's spot on the floor and note which shim goes where. While you're down there: run a nut onto each bolt and confirm you have thread to spare above it. This is the last free moment to discover you need longer bolts.

  6. Lift it off and set it aside on a towel

    Pull the shims back out. You now know exactly where everything goes.

04

The one-shot set

phase 3 · ~20 min · irreversible from step 3 on

Read this whole phase before you start it Once the bowl touches wax, the ring is committed. If you lift it, twist it hard, or land it wrong, you scrape both surfaces again and open the second ring. There is no salvage.
  1. REMOVE THE DRAIN PLUG

    Flashlight. Look. Confirm. This is the moment — after the ring goes down you can't see in there anymore.

  2. Place the ring on the flange
    ⚔ Contested — ring on the flange, or on the bowl's horn? Fluidmaster (the maker) says press it onto the bowl outlet. Oatey and Lowe's say center it on the flange. Working plumbers split down the middle. → Put it on the flange. You've already dry-fit and marked the floor, so you don't need it stuck to the bowl for alignment — and a flange-mounted ring can't fall off while you're maneuvering 55 lbs in a tight room.

    Orientation: if your ring has a plastic cone, the tapered end points DOWN into the drain, flat wide side up toward the toilet. Follow whatever's printed on your package — products differ.

    ⚔ Contested — horn or no horn? Some plumbers say the plastic funnel prevents blockages on recessed collars; others say it's the direct cause of many leaking toilets, because wax gets squeezed off the top of it and plastic seals nothing. → Flange at or above the floor: plain no-horn ring. Recessed flange or cast-iron collar: the horn earns its place.
  3. Lower the bowl — straight down

    Straddle it, lift with your legs, keep it close to your body. Lower straight down onto the bolts, using your pencil marks as the target.

    Solo method — pick one
    • Set the bolts last. If the flange slots are reachable through the base holes, land the bowl with no bolts in at all — a huge alignment margin — then drop the bolts through the holes and rotate them into the slots. Verify reachability during the dry fit, when it's free; it doesn't work on every flange.
    • Beveled wood stack. Stack tapered strips (cedar shims, beveled ply) taller than the bolts — not the ring. Land the bowl on the wood, confirm alignment through the holes, then withdraw the strips one at a time, alternating sides. Tapered is the point: they slide out under load instead of needing a lift.
    • Two drinking straws of different lengths over the bolts as tall visual guides.
    Point of no return: once the horn contacts wax — roughly the last inch — alignment is finished. Any remaining wood comes straight out. No repositioning.
  4. Compress the ring

    Steady downward body weight around the bowl until it reaches the floor.

    ⚔ Contested — twist to seat, or straight down only? Fluidmaster and Lowe's say twist slightly to spread the wax. Kohler and TOTO say do not move the bowl at all after placement. → Press straight down. No twist, no rock. It's the only motion no manufacturer forbids — and having dry-fit and marked the floor, you never needed the twist for positioning.

    The tactile check: you should feel steady, even resistance as the wax squashes, and the bowl should reach the floor. If it stops short and feels hard, something is holding it up — leftover wax, too thick a ring, a proud flange, debris. Lift it, diagnose it, and use the new ring.

    Do not lift or rock the bowl after this pointKohler states it flatly: break the seal and you must install a new one. The common way people ruin a good set is lifting or twisting the bowl later to make the tank bolts or supply line line up. Make the connections fit the toilet, not the other way around.
  5. Now seat the shims

    Slide them home by hand at your marked positions. Firm contact, no hammering.

    Order mattersIf you used the wood-stack method, the shims go in only after every piece of wood is out. Loose shims sitting on the floor first get knocked out of position — or the wood lands on top of them and changes the height.
05

Tighten

phase 4 · ~10 min · where people crack $300 of porcelain

The physics you actually need The wax seals from the weight of the toilet, not from bolt tension. The bolts only stop the bowl from shifting. Cranking harder does not improve the seal. It only breaks china.
  1. Stack the hardware in the right order

    Plastic bolt-cap base first (it's marked "up"), then the metal washer, then the nut. Get it wrong and the nut pulls straight back off.

  2. Alternate in small increments
    • Both nuts finger-tight first
    • Then alternate sides, a quarter turn or less per nut per pass
    • Use a 1/2" nut driver or a small wrench choked up near the nut. No breaker bar, no power driver. Leverage is what cracks bowls.
    • Never run one side all the way down first — that cantilevers the base and concentrates bending stress at one bolt boss
  3. Stop when the bowl stops moving

    It no longer shifts when you try to move it by hand, and it's firm on the floor and shims. That's it. That's the spec.

    There is no published torque spec — don't chase a number No major manufacturer publishes one for closet bolts. Numbers online range from 5 to 30 ft-lb — a 6× spread, which is itself proof no spec exists. As a mental ceiling only: ~5–10 ft-lb is ample and things start breaking above ~15–20. Don't go get a torque wrench: on an unevenly bearing base you'll hit the number and crack the bowl anyway. The "½ turn past hand tight" figure you'll find online is for TANK bolts. Do not apply it to the floor bolts.
  4. If it still rocks — diagnose, don't crank
    CauseFix
    Wax not fully compressedContinue tightening, small alternating increments
    Unlevel floor / uneven bearingMore shims. Not more torque. Torque will not fix this and will crack the bowl.
    One side of the base lifting off the floorSuspect a cracked flange, not a bad ring
  5. If you hear a snap — stop and check three things

    Usually it's the flat bolt head pulling up and settling into the flange slot, which is fine. But confirm: (1) does the bolt still resist an upward pull? (2) does the nut still gain purchase when you turn it — or did the threads strip? (3) inspect the base and, on an all-plastic flange, the thin area around the slot for a crack.

  6. Trim the bolts — but hold off on the caps

    If they stand more than ~1/4" above the nuts, cut the excess with the mini hacksaw. Shield the china from the blade with a putty knife or cardboard — that's Kohler's own warning.

    Leave the caps off for nowYou're going to re-check bolt snugness at 24 hours and re-test for rock at one week. Snap the caps on after that — same for snap-off bolts. Otherwise you'll be prying them back off.
06

Tank & supply

phase 5 · ~20 min · skip 1–2 if the tank never came off

  1. Tank-to-bowl gasket

    Replace it while you're in here — you rarely have the tank off. Center the gasket on the bowl inlet, set the tank down square.

  2. Tank bolts
    • Rubber against porcelain, metal washer on top of the rubber, nut last. Never seat a metal washer directly on porcelain. Follow your kit's diagram — stack order varies.
    • Hand-tight, then alternate, ~1/4 turn per nut per pass
    • Stop signal: when the rubber washers just begin to flatten. Check the tank is level side-to-side and front-to-back.
    • Tank bolts are not strain-bearing. A little give is normal.
    Never crank tank bolts to stop a tank-bolt leakA leak there means a bad washer or a washer on the wrong side. More torque cracks the tank. Kohler's manual explicitly warns overtightening breaks vitreous china.
  3. Supply line
    • No thread tape on the fill-valve nut or the compression nut at the stop — those seal with a rubber gasket, and tape prevents proper compression. (Tape IS correct on a tapered NPT thread coming out of the wall.)
    • Anti-cross-thread trick: turn the nut backward first until you feel the threads drop into engagement, then forward. It should spin easily for the first few turns. Tight early = back all the way off and restart. Never force it.
    • Fill-valve end: hand-tight only. Fluidmaster: "Do not over tighten the nut as it could damage fill valve or coupling nut, resulting in flooding and property damage."
    • Shutoff end: hand-tight plus about 1/2 turn.
    • Braided lines have a minimum bend radius — leave a gentle sweep, never a tight loop or kink, and never let the line bear weight
  4. Turn the water on SLOWLY

    Kohler: "Open the supply shut-off valve slowly to check for leaks." A slow open catches a bad joint at a trickle instead of a spray.

If the angle stop drips or won't fully close

You should have caught this back in the opening checks — but if it shows up now:

  • Confirm your main house shutoff works before touching anything
  • Tighten the packing nut 1/4 to 1/2 turn
  • Work the stem back and forth to break the mineral bond
  • A little penetrating oil where the stem enters the packing nut
  • Do not force it. Excessive force shears the stem or breaks the wall connection — that's a flood, not a drip.
  • If it still won't hold, replace it with a quarter-turn ball stop. Using push-fit (SharkBite-type)? You skip the torch, but you still need a tube cutter, a deburring/reaming tool, and a depth mark on the pipe. A burr shreds the O-ring; an under-inserted fitting blows off under pressure. Not valid on galvanized.
07

Adjust & leak test

phase 6 · ~20 min + a watch period · before you caulk

  1. Water level
    • Set the water 1/2" below the top of the overflow pipe
    • The fill valve's C.L. (Critical Level) mark must sit 1" above the top of the overflow pipe — a real backflow requirement (ASSE 1002), not a suggestion
    • Top of the fill valve ~3" above the overflow pipe puts the C.L. mark right automatically
    • Adjust while it fills: clockwise raises, counterclockwise lowers
  2. Flapper chain and refill tube

    Chain clips to the lever directly above the flapper with 1/2" of slack. Too tight runs all night; too loose weakens the flush or traps the chain under the flapper.

    Refill tube attaches to the fill-valve nipple with a slight arch into the overflow pipe. Do not shove it down inside the overflow — that siphons continuously.

  3. The paper-towel leak test
    1. Dry the floor completely
    2. Ring the entire base with dry white paper towel
    3. Flush 3–5 times over ~15 minutes, watching the base during and right after each flush
    4. Paper-towel every connection: shutoff, tank shank, tank-to-bowl joint
    5. Check again after a few hours

    Reading it: wet only after flushing → wax seal or tank-to-bowl gasket. Damp with no flushing → tank condensation or a supply drip tracking down.

    Why paperA wax weep is a few drops per flush under the momentary head of passing water — far too little to pool on the floor, but instantly obvious on dry paper.
  4. Dye-test the flapper (free, do it during the watch period)

    Ten drops of food coloring in the tank. Wait 15 minutes without flushing. Any color in the bowl means the flapper is passing — that's the phantom-refill failure, caught before you walk away.

  5. Reinstall the seat

    Hand-snug the plastic nuts. Close the lid and seat to confirm they fit and don't shift. Don't overtighten plastic hardware.

  6. At 24 hours: re-check bolts and re-test for rock — BEFORE caulking

    Gently re-snug the closet nuts and repeat the rock test. Do this now, while the joint is still open. If you caulk first and then need to retighten, you'll be cutting and redoing the bead.

08

Caulk the base

phase 7 · ~10 min + cure · code-required

Sealing the fixture-to-floor joint is required by code — IRC P2705.1.3: "Where fixtures come in contact with walls and floors, the contact area shall be water tight." This is not a matter of taste.

⚔ Contested — full perimeter, or leave a gap? For the gap: if the wax fails, water is forced out where you can see it instead of silently soaking the subfloor. Against: home inspectors point out toilets rarely leak onto the floor — they leak through it around the flange — so the gap catches a minority of failures and can give false confidence. → Caulk the front and both sides, leave a ~1" gap at the back. It satisfies the code intent (sealing against mop water, splash, and wicking — the actual smell-and-rot problem) and the gap costs nothing. But treat it as a bonus tell-tale, not your leak detection. Your real checks are the paper towel and the ceiling below.
  1. Trim the shims flush first

    Score with a sharp utility knife and snap — one firm stroke. Cut at a slight angle so the edges aren't visible. Hard plastic shims snap cleanly with a firm chisel hit (eye protection).

  2. Prep the joint — silicone won't bond to a wet dusty floor

    Dry it completely, wipe with denatured alcohol, then mask both sides with tape for a clean bead.

  3. Run the bead, tool it, pull the tape immediately

    100% silicone — not latex, not acrylic. Never grout or mortar: that makes the next removal, in the trade's words, "a horrible job that sometimes involves breaking the toilet." Note that 100% silicone cannot be painted.

    Your shims are in the gapShim edges sit at the back — the same place you're leaving open. Put a dab of silicone over each cut face so the trimmed edge is sealed even though the joint there isn't.

Wax vs. waxless — the decision, honestly

→ Recommendation: traditional wax ring. Buy two.

You've committed to dry-fit-first, which already eliminates the main thing waxless seals solve (repositionability). Wax conforms to any gap and any imperfect flange face. Waxless seals have a fixed minimum compressed height and can leave the bowl hovering 1/4"–1/2" off the floor if the geometry isn't right — a worse day than a smeared wax ring, and a second wax ring costs $4.

Go waxless (Fluidmaster 7530, Korky 6000, Sani Seal BL01) only if you expect to pull this toilet again soon and your flange is level and at or near correct height. Reported failures cluster where the flange sits above floor level — the seal can't flatten enough. They also add ~1/8" of height.

Oatey's own copy admits waxless "may not seal well on uneven or damaged surfaces" and have "unproven longevity." Foam types have a documented failure mode: the outer layer delaminates, the foam wicks and holds water against the flange, and the closet bolts rust off.

The single most common wrong-part purchaseFluidmaster 7513 is NOT waxless. It's an extra-thick wax ring with a plastic funnel, for flanges even with or below the floor. The waxless Fluidmaster is 7530.

11

The five that actually matter

if you remember nothing else

  1. Measure the flange before you buy the ring

    Flush to 1/4" proud is correct. Everything downstream depends on this one number.

  2. Dry-fit with no wax. Find the rock. Mark it. Shim it.

    Then lift it off and set it for real. This single habit prevents most of the failures on this page.

  3. Pull the drain plug before the ring goes down

    Flashlight. Confirm with your eyes, not your memory.

  4. Straight down, full body weight, then stop touching it

    Once it's on wax, it's committed. Make the connections fit the toilet, not the other way around.

  5. Alternate quarter-turns until the movement stops, then quit

    The weight seals it; the bolts just hold it. There is no number to hit.

Time & manpower 1.5–2.5 hours picking up from a toilet already off the floor — budget the afternoon. A two-piece bowl alone is 50–60 lbs, well within one adult's capacity. The problem was never the weight; it's lowering a heavy glazed object straight down onto two bolts you can't see, in one motion, without twisting. Solo is entirely doable with the bolts-last or beveled-wood method plus your pencil marks. If you do have a helper: their job is to kneel and sight the bolts through the base holes, calling left/right/forward/back — not to help lift. Two people lifting one bowl is how it goes in crooked.
