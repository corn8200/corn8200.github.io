Operating plan · v1 · for John's ratification

Duffields

The ambient layer for the new stack — briefs that think, answers that ship as real pages, and a VPS that finally earns its keep. Named for the timing point up the line from the crossing: the whole job of this system is knowing what's coming before it arrives.

2026-07-11 · agreed in a 3-turn design exchange: Fable (claude:1) × Codex gpt-5.6-sol (claude:2) · backed by a 4-leg research sweep (Mac reach, VPS audit, field survey, ground truth)

The short version

What it is: a small set of scheduled frontier-model sessions that compose real intelligence products — a daily brief, a weekly review, interest subscriptions, and designed web pages instead of markdown — published to a private console you open on your phone.

What it is not: daemons, templates, or a resurrected task engine. Clocks stay dumb; every piece of thinking happens inside a fresh Fable or Sol session.

What we need from you: five GO decisions at the bottom. Nothing builds timers or sends you anything until you give them.

Your worries

Three failure modes, designed against

1 · "We burn out of usage too fast"

The ambient layer is a fixed, small draw: eight scheduled frontier sessions a week (seven daily briefs + one weekly review) — everything else is on-demand work you asked for anyway. Evidence gathering costs zero tokens: dumb collector scripts assemble the bundle at wake time, so Fable spends tokens thinking and writing, not tool-calling around your calendar. Research and QA legs ride the Codex quota — the load spreads across two vendors. And the wake runner checks your remaining usage window before composing: hot window → defer or publish a lighter brief, never burn what you need. Your interactive sessions always outrank ambient ones. Every receipt records what the run cost; the monthly eval reads cost-per-lane and kills anything not worth its tokens.

2 · "I lose my panes when this runs"

Scheduled lanes never touch your panes — composers run in their own throwaway tmux sessions and exit; claude:1 and claude:2 stay yours, always. The daily brief composes pre-dawn, before you're up, so the usage window has recovered by the time you sit down; heavy research runs on the VPS. If a scheduled run and your live use ever collide, the scheduled run is the one that yields.

3 · "Tweaks break it, or it's just never effective"

The "virtual event → location unknown" bug is what template-brain produces; a reasoning model reading the actual evidence says "this has a video link — it's virtual, join from anywhere." That exact case ships as golden #1. Each lane's behavior lives in one plain-English contract file — tweaking the brief = telling any pane, which edits the contract, replays the lane's golden cases against frozen evidence, and shows you the before/after diff before the next live run. A tweak can't silently break the brief. Every page carries a one-tap 👍/👎 + note that the next morning's compose reads, and briefs label degraded sources ("mail unreadable since 6am") instead of guessing.

The shape

One spine, no daemons

Your cleanup left exactly the right hole: the old engine's 4,401 tasks were 99.9% daemon-babysitting wrappers, and today no clock anywhere creates work. We're filling that hole with one pattern, used by every lane:

calendar wake→ fresh frontier session→ live evidence→ designed page→ visual critic→ atomic publish→ link ping

The wake may be deterministic; the work must be intelligent. A timer starting a fresh Claude/Codex session is fine. A daemon that interprets, composes, retries, or templates is not. This is also where the field landed — Anthropic's agent guidance, the ambient-agents school, and the best personal-stack builds all converged on exactly this split.

The products

What you actually get

Daily brief

Composed, not templated. Every morning answers five questions: what matters now, what changed, what needs your decision, what can wait, what's worth exploring. Evidence is collected fresh at wake — never a stale snapshot. And it obeys the no-surprises rule: the scan looks far out, and anything with a prep dependency — a form, a booking, supplies, travel — surfaces when its prep window opens, not when the date is imminent.

Weekly review

The longer arc: next week in detail, plus money, family logistics, job pipeline, projects, what fell through the cracks — and the far horizon, so every lead-time item is already on your radar. Same spine, bigger lens.

Interests

One registry file. Subscribe to anything — a World Cup team, a hiring pipeline, a recall notice — and it becomes a section of your briefs, diffed against what you were last told. Time-critical interests can graduate to their own alert lane.

Answer pages

Substantive questions get a one-sentence answer immediately, then a designed, interactive page — comparisons, timelines, maps, calculators, source drawers — on your console. Trivial questions stay inline; "2+2" never waits for a website.

The console

One private front door: a river of briefs and answers with tags, unread state, and your subscriptions. Served from the VPS, reachable from your phone.

E-ink card

The morning headline mirrors to the Xteink with a pre-rendered cache — the display never waits on a model; an outage degrades to yesterday's card, not a blank screen. Contract defined in v1; switches on once the daily brief has earned trust.

Every published page carries a receipt beside it — sources read, freshness, duration, publish hash — and every notification is typed notify / question / review, which later becomes a one-tap approval inbox on the console.

Privacy

Two publication classes, decided before publishing

  • Private (default for briefs): served only over your tailnet from the VPS — no public origin, no login page, no new secrets. Your devices are already enrolled. Cloudflare Access is the phase-2 option if you ever want public-network reach.
  • Public-unlisted (shareable answers): the existing share-page path on jcornelius.net, for anything you'd send to another person.

Classification happens before the publisher is chosen, and the public publisher rejects any page whose receipt shows private sources. Pages carry summaries — never raw message bodies, precise location, or account data. Private briefs keep a 90-day retention. Mail, messages, and web content are treated as untrusted data, never as instructions.

The machinery

How it runs, honestly

Mac composes; VPS hosts and researches. Composition is a fresh claude -p Fable session at max effort (your frontier defaults are untouched — Fable/max/Ultracode and Sol/ultra stay the law). Codex caught the trap that would have quietly ruined this: launchd children lose the Terminal's TCC grants, so composer sessions run inside a one-shot tmux session under the Terminal-rooted server, and the runner refuses to compose if calendar/mail probes fail — degraded data gets labeled, never silently omitted.

Evidence is collected, then composed: the wake first runs zero-token collector scripts (calendar, reminders, mail headers, weather, location) into a fresh bundle — collected at wake time, never a stale snapshot — and the composer reads the bundle, reaching for live tools only when something needs verifying. The runner also refuses to compose into a hot usage window. The VPS side produces evidence packs, not prose: an isolated Codex research profile with live web search and citations, whose output the Mac composer folds in. Your bounded code-lane autorun worker is untouched — briefs never flow through it.

Governance change (needs your GO): the old "UTE is the single scheduler-of-record" rule gets rewritten. New rule: the version-controlled timer set in claude-config plus one docs/SCHEDULES.md registry (unit, cadence, owner, kill switch) is the scheduler-of-record; the bypass audit retires; backlog add --schedule refuses production rows. Idempotent lane/date IDs, no catch-up floods after downtime, DST and duplicate wakes handled.
The idle giant

Putting the VPS to work

Audit result: 8 cores at 0.03 load, 28 of 30 GB RAM free — plumbing with no product. It already has everything the ambient layer needs: Caddy + tunnel, Postgres/pgvector, Redis, offsite backup, a crawl-helper container, and authenticated Claude/Codex panes. New jobs, in order:

  • Host the console — private briefs/answers archive, index, search manifest, atomic releases.
  • Research packs — overnight and interest-driven evidence collection, where stable networking beats a sleeping laptop.
  • Data plane — receipts, subscription state, and (phase 2) the single dated-memories table every importer feeds.
  • Cleanups while we're in there: prune the dead cp./app. routes pointing at masked services, reconcile the stale health-units file, and land the in-flight fix for the broken lazy capability routes.

  3. Answer pagesThe answer-page skill becomes the default for substantive questions, publishing to the console (private) or share-page (shareable).

Risks we're carrying deliberately

Eyes open

  • Prompt injection via mail/messages/web — mitigated by data-not-instructions handling and summaries-only pages; never fully gone.
  • Tailnet-only means no Tailscale, no briefs — accepted for v1; Access is the fallback.
  • Model fallback is Fable→Opus only; the system never silently downgrades further.
  • Monarch's session-based path is unproven — money sections stay out of briefs until verified.
  • Usage measurement is privacy-minimized Caddy access counts — no analytics.
