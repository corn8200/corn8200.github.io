Setup plan · for Aunt

Make the spare MacBook a simple, safe machine for Aunt

Photos from the family that just show up, and safe browsing & email — with the trapdoors that let a very non-tech user break things (or get scammed) closed off. Follow it in order.

~45 minutes $0 if she has wifi No admin skills needed
Do this first — it decides everything

A MacBook has no ethernet port — it needs wifi. She had internet, "maybe just LAN." Before anything else, find out what her internet box actually offers (Step 1). And confirm the service is still active — the internet is separate from her dead PC, so it may still be on, or may have lapsed.

1

Get it online

The whole plan is dead unless the Mac can reach the internet over wifi.

  • Look at her internet box (the modem/router the ISP left). If there's a wifi network name + password on a sticker, you're set — connect from the wifi menu (top-right of the screen).
  • Wired-only, no wifi? Two cheap fixes: a small wifi router (~$20–40), or a USB-C → Ethernet adapter (~$15) and keep the Mac plugged in at a desk. For someone who sits in one spot, wired is fine and more reliable.
  • Confirm the ISP bill is still being paid — the service can outlive the old computer.
2

Update it & check what you're working with

A 6-year-old Mac is either great-for-years or nearing its last OS update — worth knowing which.

  • Apple menu → About This Mac. If the chip says Apple M1/M2, you're golden for years. If it says Intel, it still works fine but is near the end of new macOS updates — not a dealbreaker, just don't sink money into it.
  • Run System Settings → General → Software Update and let it finish before you set anything up.
3

Two accounts — the single biggest safety move

A non-admin account is what makes the machine scam-resistant and hard to break.

  • Keep one account as your admin account, password only you know.
  • System Settings → Users & Groups → Add Account → make Aunt a Standard user (not Administrator).
  • Why it matters: a Standard user can't install software, change system settings, or be talked into "download this to fix your computer." That one setting blocks most malware and tech-support scams cold.
4

Give her one Apple ID

A single free account powers both the photos and the email — no juggling.

  • Create a free Apple ID for her (it comes with an @icloud.com email address).
  • Sign into it on her Standard account. Write the login down somewhere you'll both find it.
5

Family photos that just appear

This is the "GrandPad magic" part — she does nothing, pictures show up.

  • On your iPhone or Mac: Photos → Shared Album → create one ("Family") and invite her Apple ID.
  • Anyone in the family drops photos into that album; they land in her Photos app automatically. No action from her, ever.
  • Bonus: set that shared album as her screen saver — family photos just cycle when the Mac is idle.
6

Email, kept simple

Email is the #1 way scams reach seniors — set it up plain and give her one rule.

  • Open Mail, signed in with her iCloud address. Turn on junk filtering.
  • The one rule to tell her: never type a password or card number because an email told you to. Real companies don't ask that way.
7

Browsing with guardrails

Browsing is the other risky surface — bookmark the good, block the bad.

  • Open Safari, visit her sites, and bookmark them big on the start page (news, weather, whatever she reads).
  • On her account: System Settings → Screen Time → Content & Privacy → Web Content → limit adult/scam sites.
  • Install an ad-blocker — it kills most of the fake "your computer is infected!" popups before she ever sees them.
8

Make it easy on the eyes and hands

Small text and a cluttered Dock are what make older users feel lost.

  • Bigger everything: System Settings → Displays → choose a "Larger Text" scaling; and Accessibility → Display → increase text + pointer size so the cursor is easy to find.
  • Clean the Dock down to ~4 icons: Photos, Safari, Mail, Messages. Drag the rest off.
  • Turn on auto-login (Users & Groups → Automatically log in as → her account) so there's no login screen to fumble. Skip only if you want a password.
  • Turn on Accessibility → Reduce Motion for a calmer, less disorienting screen.
9

Remote help so you never have to drive over

This replaces GrandPad's "family manages it" feature — you fix things from your couch.

  • Your setup (best, free): install Tailscale on her Mac and add it to your tailnet, then enable System Settings → General → Sharing → Screen Sharing. You connect from anywhere and she does nothing.
  • Simpler route: AnyDesk or TeamViewer (free for personal use) with unattended access set up — same idea, no clicking on her end.
The one 2-minute talk that matters

The Standard account already blocks most real damage. Give her exactly two habits:

1. Nobody real will ever call or pop up about your computer. Hang up / close it.

2. Never type a password or card number because a message told you to.

Then drop a big note or bookmark on screen: "Confused? Call John."

When to bail on the MacBook

It's the free option and it'll work — but two situations make something else the smarter call.

No easy way to get her online

A real GrandPad ships with its own cellular data — no router, no wifi, nothing to set up. ~$25–40/mo via Consumer Cellular or Amazon. Worth it purely to skip the connectivity headache.

The laptop itself frustrates her

If the trackpad and windows trip her up, an iPad with Assistive Access is the closest true GrandPad — giant buttons, locked to a few apps, far easier to lock down than a Mac. Reuse an old one or buy a cheap base iPad.
