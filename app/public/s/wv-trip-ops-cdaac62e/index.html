Tonight's town

Durbin — where a river is born

The Durbin & Greenbrier Valley Railroad has run excursions from this depot since 1996, and the rail yard's treasure is Climax No. 3, built 1910 — one of only three operating Climax locomotives left on Earth (this summer's Durbin Rocket runs behind Heisler No. 6, on loan from Cass — cousins visiting). Pamphlet · stop 1

Cass Scenic Railroad — running since 1901

Cass was built in 1901 by West Virginia Pulp & Paper as a company town — mill, store, school, and the white houses still standing — to cut red spruce off these mountains. The locomotives are geared Shays: pistons spin a driveshaft geared to every wheel, all-wheel-drive in low gear, which is how they walk up 11% grades (with two switchbacks) from the depot at 2,452 ft to Whittaker Station at 3,264 ft. Up top: a recreated 1940s logging camp.

Shay No. 5, built November 1905, is the oldest operating Shay on Earth — she has worked this exact line her entire 120-year life, and in 2004 West Virginia made her the official state steam locomotive.

Tell the boys: regular trains are ice skaters on a hill — they spin and slip. A Shay is a mountain goat: every wheel grabs. That's why she can climb straight up this mountain. 🐐🚂

Be aboard-ready 10:30 · no restrooms on the train · your snacks/water ride along · runs rain or shine

Pamphlet · stop 2

Green Bank — the ear that listens to space

The Green Bank Telescope is the largest fully steerable radio telescope in the world: 485 feet tall — 180 feet taller than the Statue of Liberty — with a 100×110-meter dish covering 2.34 acres (almost two football fields), and the whole 17-million-pound instrument rolls on 16 wheels around a 210-foot circular track to aim anywhere in the sky. The National Radio Quiet Zone (est. 1958) keeps 13,000 square miles hushed for it — Green Bank's ~143 residents live basically without cell service on purpose.

Tell the boys: the signals it catches from space are so faint that a single snowflake landing on the ground gives off more energy. That's why everybody's phones have to whisper here. ❄️📡

Free · exhibit hall + observation deck · WiFi & Bluetooth OFF · phone photos OK at the science center only

Pamphlet · stop 3

Spruce Knob — the roof of West Virginia

4,863 feet — the state's highest point. The Forest Service's stone-and-steel tower rises over one-sided red spruces "deformed by constant exposure to strong westerly winds" (the Forest Service's own words — every branch points east, like flags frozen mid-wave). The half-mile Whispering Spruce loop circles the summit with the big views.

Tell the boys: up here you're standing as high above the ocean as 3⅓ Empire State Buildings stacked on top of each other. And look at the trees — the wind combed all their branches to one side! 🏔️

Vault toilets, no water · picnic tables · 10–15° cooler and windy — hoodies on

Free bonus · right on the road

Seneca Rocks — the 900-foot fin

Minutes after the summit road rejoins US-33, you drive straight past Seneca Rocks — a razorback fin of 440-million-year-old Tuscarora quartzite rising ~900 feet off the valley floor, one of WV's most famous sights and a rock-climbing mecca. Gawk from the car, or give it ten minutes from the visitor-center lawn if the crew's awake.

Tonight's village

Helvetia — a piece of Switzerland, population 38

Founded in 1869 by Swiss immigrants who came via Brooklyn, Helvetia never stopped being Swiss: the whole village is a National Register historic district (1978), famous for Fasnacht — the pre-Lent festival where the town parades in carved masks and then burns an effigy of Old Man Winter (the masks survive; winter doesn't). Gamers know the place too: the village and its Fasnacht are lovingly recreated in Fallout 76 (the Hütte appears as "Freyja's Restaurant").

Best part: you're sleeping inside the landmark — the Kultur Haus, with the mask museum, general store, and tiny post office downstairs from your suite.

Tell the boys: only 38 people live in this whole village — when your crew rolls in, you make it 42! 🎭

Hütte closed Mon · store hours: ask at check-in · offline maps — GPS fibs out here

The finale

The Flatwoods Monster — September 12, 1952

That evening a fireball — a meteor tracked across Maryland, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia — blazed over Braxton County. Kathleen May, her sons, and a pack of local boys hiked up a dark hillside to find where it landed… and met something they described as 10 to 12 feet tall with a glowing face and a spade-shaped head. The country went wild for it. Fifty years later, investigator Joe Nickell showed the "monster" matched a startled barn owl rearing up on a branch — but by then Flatwoods had adopted her forever as Braxie, the friendliest monster in Appalachia. The museum (full-scale since 2017) is free, and the county hid five giant Braxie chairs for hunters like your crew.

Tell the boys: a space rock zoomed over, everyone ran up the dark hill, and a mama owl guarding her nest reared up big and hissed — and in the flashlights she looked TEN FEET TALL (that's exactly as tall as a basketball hoop). The mystery got solved, but the town kept the monster as a friend. 👾🦉

Tue 9–5 · free · chairs A/C/D are on your route; all five = free Braxie sticker
