Getting Your Gun Rights Back

A step-by-step plan to lift a 25-year-old federal conviction off your back — and put you back in the deer woods with your own rifle.

Yes. This is fixable.

Your case was federal — we confirmed it in the Bureau of Prisons' own records — so the fix comes from Washington, not a county courthouse. That makes it a patient play, not a quick one. But here's the part that matters: the government just restarted, for the first time in 33 years, a program built to give gun rights back to people with old, non-violent convictions — and its own rulebook describes its ideal candidate as someone with a decades-old, comparatively minor, non-violent felony and a clean life since. That's you, almost word for word. We file two applications, both from the kitchen table, and both are basically free.

What we already know about your case

  • Federal case, southern West Virginia. Bureau of Prisons register number 05773-088 — the “088” is the code for the Southern District of West Virginia (the Huntington federal court, right by Milton). Released June 16, 2000.
  • The boot camp works in your favor. The federal boot camp at Lewisburg, Pennsylvania took only volunteers: minimum-security, non-violent men, 35 or under, first time inside, short sentences. You had to be judged low-risk just to get in — and finishing a 180-day, 5 a.m.-to-lights-out military program is written proof of who you chose to become. It goes straight into the application.
  • A quarter century clean since. No trace of you in any court system anywhere in the country after that case — we searched. That clean stretch is the single strongest thing in the file.

Why the state courthouse can't fix this

Everyone will tell you “just get it expunged.” That works for West Virginia cases — but a WV judge has no power over a federal judgment, and the U.S. Supreme Court has said flat-out that for a federal conviction, only federal relief lifts the federal gun ban. So the play runs through the Department of Justice, and once Washington says yes, one simple petition at the Lewisburg courthouse cleans up the West Virginia side. Anyone who promises you a quick state-court fix — or a lawsuit — is selling something; the courts in our part of the country have shut the lawsuit door, and the applications below don't need a lawyer at all.
Beecham v. United States (1994); 18 U.S.C. § 921(a)(20); WV Code § 61-7-7.

The plan, step by step

Six steps. John handles paperwork and phone calls; your part is telling the truth, signing your name, and picking three people who'll vouch for you.

1

Get your records — free Together, this weekend

One signature from you on a one-page identity form (DOJ-361 — John's bringing it printed) lets us request your complete Bureau of Prisons file at no charge: the judgment, your sentence paperwork, and the boot-camp completion record. John will also call the federal clerk in Huntington for your case number, and order the official indictment-and-judgment packet from the federal archives — a flat $35.

Huntington federal clerk: 304-529-5588 (845 Fifth Ave, Room 101). Always use your full name and date of birth — Dad shares your name, and we keep the records straight by birth date.
2

Check the file for one thing Together

The new gun-rights program presumes a “no” for a short list of cases — violent crimes, sex offenses, recent drug or domestic-violence cases, people still on paper. You're none of those. The one wrinkle to rule out from the old paperwork: whether any of the stolen goods in the case were guns — that's the only version of your offense on their caution list. Straight answer this weekend; the records will confirm it.

3

Pick your three Jimmy's job

Both applications want letters from three people who know you well, aren't family by blood or marriage, have known you three-plus years, and would say to an FBI agent's face that you're solid. Think employer or foreman, pastor, landowner whose ground you hunt, longtime neighbor. They write plain, honest letters — no lawyer language. This is the one job only you can do, and it's the part the government weighs most: steady work, family, and what your community says about you.

4

File the pardon application — free John drafts · You sign

A presidential pardon wipes the conviction's effects — gun rights come back under federal law and West Virginia law recognizes an unconditional pardon automatically. The application is a government form, filed by email, no fee, no notary — you sign under penalty of perjury and it has to be the whole truth, including every scrape you've ever had, down to old traffic stuff. The story writes itself: one mistake at twenty-something, volunteered for the hardest program the system offered, then twenty-five years of work and family. Fair warning: the pardon office is buried and stingy right now — this one's the lottery ticket. It costs nothing, there's no penalty for trying, and if they say no you can refile immediately. So we play it — but we don't wait on it.

5

File the gun-rights application — the real lane John's job

The Department of Justice's restarted restoration program is the one actually built for your case. The final rule and application form are expected to open soon; the proposed fee is $20, waived if money's tight, and the government has said it plans to decide applications within about a year. We'll have your whole packet — records, letters, fingerprint cards — sitting ready so it goes in the day the window opens. Meanwhile, the same office has already restored rights for 36 people who simply wrote in before any form existed — so your paperwork goes in front of them now, by email, at zero cost.

6

When Washington says yes: one trip to Lewisburg Together

Federal relief in hand, we file a short petition at the Greenbrier County courthouse — your home county — to clear the West Virginia layer. $200 filing fee, or $0 with a fee waiver. West Virginia's own supreme court has said judges can't grant this while the federal ban still stands, which is exactly why we do it last. Then get several certified copies of everything, and go buy the rifle. Expect the store's background check to come back “delayed” the first time while the computers catch up — that's normal and fixable, not a problem.

This fall — already legal, starting now

  • Archery & crossbow deer season: Sept 26 – Dec 31, 2026. Bows and crossbows are not “firearms” under federal or West Virginia law — the conviction doesn't touch them. You can buy a bow tomorrow and hunt the whole season, fully legal.
  • Big-bore air rifle. WV allows air rifles .45-cal and up (200-grain bullet minimum) for deer during firearms seasons — and air rifles aren't legally firearms either. A modern PCP air gun drops deer clean inside 75 yards.
  • Old-style muzzleloaders — legal on paper, but careful. Both federal and WV law genuinely exclude traditional black-powder guns from the word “firearm,” and the ATF itself says a person in your position may possess one. If you go that road: flintlock or percussion-cap sidelock only — never a modern inline that takes 209 shotgun primers, because those primers count as regular ammunition, which stays off-limits. The bulletproof play is bow, crossbow, or air rifle until the letters come.

Until then — the don't list

These keep the plan safe. Discipline now, freedom after.

  • No guns and no ammunition under your control — the ammo part surprises people: a single loose shotgun shell in the truck is its own federal felony.
  • “Not mine” doesn't help if it's in your space. Guns in your house, your truck, or a camp you control can be charged as yours. If others in the household own guns, they stay locked up, someone else keeps the key.
  • Don't hold, borrow, or haul anyone's gun — even carrying a buddy's rifle back to the truck counts as possession.
  • At family hunts, you're the bow guy this year. Not forever.
