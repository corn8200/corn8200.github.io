Position summary

Serves as the Cybersecurity Modernization Branch's automation specialist for governance, risk, and compliance operations. This is a process-engineering and applied-AI position embedded in a cybersecurity mission: the incumbent industrializes the branch's GRC workload — analyzing manual compliance workflows, engineering data pipelines and AI-assisted tooling that reduce cycle time and manual labor, and building the governance that keeps those tools accurate, auditable, and trusted. The incumbent partners with ISSOs, assessors, and system owners who own the cybersecurity judgment; the incumbent owns the automation that carries the workload.

Major duties

1 · GRC process analysis and re-engineering — 25%

Applies Lean Six Sigma methodology to the branch's manual governance, risk, and compliance workflows — evidence collection, control selection, implementation-statement drafting, assessment preparation, and POA&M upkeep. Produces current-state/future-state value-stream maps with system owners and ISSOs, identifies waste and rework, and delivers re-engineered workflows with measured reductions in cycle time and manual touch labor. Facilitates improvement events with stakeholders and documents the redesigned processes as standard work.

2 · AI-assisted compliance tooling (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) — 25%

Designs, builds, and operates AI-powered tools that assist compliance tasks: retrieval of control language, policy, and evidence with forced citations; first-draft generation of control-implementation statements and recurring compliance narratives for ISSO review and ownership; and rapid, cited answers to assessor questions. Engineers the supporting retrieval infrastructure — corpus curation from authoritative sources, chunking and indexing, metadata and access filtering, hybrid keyword-plus-semantic search for exact identifiers such as control IDs — and maintains evaluation sets that are re-run on every corpus or model change.

3 · Data integration and evidence pipeline engineering — 15%

Builds and maintains the data plumbing that automation depends on: connectors and pipelines that pull compliance-relevant evidence directly from source systems in structured form, eliminating manual transcription and screenshot-collection; schemas, data dictionaries, and validation rules that keep ingested data consistent and traceable to its source and version.

4 · Compliance documentation as code — 10%

Converts compliance artifacts from static prose toward machine-readable, version-controlled formats (e.g., OSCAL, JSON/YAML) maintained under configuration control; builds tooling that generates, validates, and updates documentation from authoritative data, version-stamps every artifact, and retires superseded versions so stale guidance cannot circulate.

5 · AI model governance — 10%

Establishes and supports the governance that makes AI tooling trustworthy and auditable: grounding and citation requirements, refusal behavior on empty retrieval, human ownership of consequential outputs, per-user access enforcement at retrieval, query/answer logging, evaluation gates before and after every change, and entry of AI tools into the organization's use-case inventory and assessment processes — ensuring the branch's automation meets the same standards it helps enforce.

6 · Compliance metrics, dashboards, and drift visibility — 10%

Builds dashboards and recurring reporting that give branch leadership continuous visibility into compliance posture and workload: cycle times, backlog and POA&M aging, documentation currency, and divergence between documented and actual system state. Automates recurring reports that are currently assembled by hand.

7 · Adoption, training, and standards authorship — 5%

Drives adoption of new workflows and tools: writes the standard operating procedures and playbooks, trains ISSOs and branch staff, collects user feedback into the improvement cycle, and authors the branch's automation standards so results outlast any single tool or person.

Suggested qualification framing

  • The scarce competency is the automation, not the framework. The labor market has no shortage of RMF-fluent compliance staff; it has an acute shortage of people who have shipped production AI and process automation inside regulated, safety-critical operations. Defining the specialized experience as "applying process re-engineering, data integration, and AI/ML tooling to compliance or regulated-operations workflows" describes what these duties actually require — and what is genuinely hard to hire.
  • Framework knowledge is trainable in-house. The branch is rich in RMF and Coast Guard context; duties above deliberately place cybersecurity judgment with ISSOs and assessors, and automation delivery with the incumbent. Working knowledge of RMF/NIST sufficient to partner effectively is the honest requirement — expert IA experience is not what the seat does.
  • Every duty above is evidenced at the production level, not the familiarity level — the basis for a superior-qualifications determination if HR pursues one at the offer stage.
